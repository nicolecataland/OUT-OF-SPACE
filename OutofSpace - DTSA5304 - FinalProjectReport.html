Out of Space

A visual exploration of space object re-entries.

DTSA 5304 Final Project

Pre-Launch Briefing

*project motivation*

Living on the Space Coast in Florida, I've been privileged to get to see first hand the rapid advancement of the space industry in recent years. Opportunities to witness rocket launches have gone from several times a year to several times a week. However, while this increase in the number of objects being launched into space has been widely reported, far less attention has been given to the increase of objects falling back down from space.

All objects in orbit are being pulled back down towards Earth by Earth's gravity.* The high speeds at which these objects are inserted into orbit allow them to counter this effect and stay in space by moving fast enough to keep "falling around" the planet. Over time, though, tiny amounts of air resistance in the upper atmosphere cause these objects to slowly lose speed, and consequently, altitude.

Most objects burn up completely due to atmospheric friction as they descend, but sometimes larger or sturdier pieces survive and impact the ground. Just last year, a 11cm discarded battery component released from the International Space Station crashed through the roof and floor of a Naples, Florida home. Fortunately no one was injured, but the incident demonstrated how even a relatively small object can cause considerable damage.

Reentries can be controlled, meaning engineers plan them carefully and aim for safe places like oceans. But many—like the discarded ISS component—are uncontrolled, making it hard to predict exactly where leftover pieces might land.

(*Technically there's never a point in space where the gravitational effect of Earth on an object is zero, no matter how far out you go. But that's another conversation.)

Mission Objective

*visualization goal*

My goal for this final project was to help close the aforementioned knowledge gap by developing a data visualization for a general audience that would educate and bring awareness to space object reentry trends. This target user group may not have technical expertise, but are everyday people with curiosity about space exploration, science communicators who want to explain events, and those concerned about the future of space activity and its potential impacts on Earth.

Payload Manifest

*about the data*

Though humble in appearance, Jonathan's Space Pages is a rich online resource for space-related data, curated by Dr. Jonathan McDowell, a British-American astrophysicist at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics. His website offers a range of detailed and regularly updated datasets, all generously made available free for public use. Of particular interest is the Space Object Reentries Catalog, which meticulously documents the reentries of artificial space objects since the dawn of the space age.

The unpretentious homepage of the Jonathan's Space Pages website.

This dataset includes important temporal information about the launch date and subsequent re-entry date (which will be critical to identify data trends), in addition to other interesting qualitative and quantitative attributes, such as the country or organization responsible for the object, the mass of the object upon reentry, the object type (payload, rocket body, etc.), and disposal/de-orbit method used.

While the richness of this dataset opens up many visualization possibilities, I had to take care to stay focused on the needs of the target audience: the general public. Many of the data attributes, like the disposal/de-orbit method codes, are highly technical and would likely fail to convey any meaningful information to non-expert users*. Most people likely only care to know: Was the space object reentry controlled or uncontrolled? To better serve this audience, some data simplification and restructuring was necessary before it would be ready for visualization. (More on that later.)

Visual Flight Plan 

*the what and why*

With the goal in mind and data in hand, I was then able to proceed with outlining the visualization tasks. As previously mentioned, my objective was to create an educational data visualization about space object reentries. In terms of task-driven design, the intended purpose is largely presentation and enjoyment, though I hoped to accommodate data exploration as well by including interactive elements and filtering in the design.

*the who and when*

Designing a visualization for such purposes liberated me to be a bit more creative than if I was designing a visualization to help facilitate a specific business function ( ..though still obligated to present the data as honestly and faithfully as possible, of course!) That is not to say however that it would be without challenge. My attempts to elicit visualization tasks from peers did not come up too fruitful. Most individuals were not familiar enough with space object reentries to even know the questions they would want to ask.  That being said, this effort did indicate to me that my target users would probably not be as interested in aspects like object’s type or orbit as they are to know simply how big the object is.

Researching existing space object re-entries visualizations also yielded only line and bar charts. While this type of visualization does effectively illustrate the reentry temporal trends I wanted to communicate, I wasn't confident it would hold my target user's attention. This made me determined to come up with a way to present the data that was more visually compelling and engaged the user.

*the how and where*

Since I was assuming my target audience had no prior knowledge about space object reentries, I knew I would need to contextualize my data. I took a great deal of inspiration from digital publications like The Pudding, that use "scrollytelling" techniques to tell data-driven stories. Employing a similar strategy would allow me to provide a narrative that would guide the user's action and navigation through the data, without overwhelming the user with too much information at once. I determined that using time as an absolute reference for my data would be the most conducive frame for this approach. I also knew that an object's mass and disposal/de-orbit method would need to be reflected in the visualization, as these were the attributes most closely associated with a re-entering space object's risk factor.

Final Countdown

*constructing the visualization*

Though the tasks I had outlined earlier served as an accurate guidepost throughout the development of my visualization, there were still many scrapped design sheets and prototypes involved before reaching the final design implementation. For one thing, I am much more comfortable in a Python development environment, but it was quickly evident that in order to implement the dynamic visualization I had been envisioning, I would need something that would allow for more flexibility. “Always use the right tool for the job” is advice that has yet to steer me wrong, and so I landed on D3 (a Javascript library) to get it done.

Some early (and fortunately abandoned) design ideas for my visualization.

Another challenge I encountered during development was that I had been too ambitious in trying to maintain my data’s granularity. Inspired by data visualizations like this one, I had sought to retain and display all of the individual object data points in my visualization. This resulted in overly cluttered early prototypes with terrible load time and performance.

I ultimately was able to resolve this issue by opting to filter out all objects with a mass below the threshold that would trigger a U.S. Space Command Public Reentry Tracking Alert (3000kg).

I also discovered in this process that including the launch date of the space object in my visualization design wasn’t adding any meaningful knowledge or insight opportunities to the graphic. Instead just plotting the reentry date of the object resulted in a much cleaner, easy to intuit design.

Liftoff

*key elements of the final visualization design*

The final design is an interactive timeline, supplemented with narrative cards that provide context as the user navigates through the data. To further support this narrative flow, I oriented the timeline vertically instead of along the more common x-axis. This made for a more natural and intuitive scrolling experience for the user exploring the data.

Screen recording of the space object reentry timeline visualization.

The user is able to hover over any data point in the visualization to reveal a tooltip containing all of the reentry details of that object, including its country of origin and if it was a controlled or uncontrolled reentry. The size of each data point in the visualization directly corresponds to that object’s reentry mass. With our lecture on ethical visualization practices in mind, I took great care to ensure that the mass of the object mapped to the area of the circle and not the diameter to avoid misrepresenting the data.  

Systems Review

*design evaluation*

Given my visualization purpose was largely for presentation and enjoyment, I determined the design evaluation would lend itself better to the more informal methodologies, over something like a controlled lab study. However to make sure the evaluation was nonetheless rigorous, I opted for an approach that combined quantitative and qualitative measures.

I conducted an observational study on my participant users by observing how they interacted with and navigated through the visualization, which definitely didn’t make them uncomfortable at all. (The observational study was also convenient given the visualization is hosted locally on my laptop.) I also asked one participant to “think aloud” as they engaged with the visualization to see if it would reveal additional insight. Additionally, I conducted post-session interviews with all participants to solicit any thoughts, questions, and impressions they had after using the tool. Lastly, to have a quantitative measure, I gave all participants a 3 question survey that asked the user to rate on a scale of 1 to 5: how engaging they found the visualization, how easy it was to understand the visualization, and if they felt they gained new knowledge through the visualization.

Mission Accomplished

*evaluation results*

Though the inherent bias in my participant user selection threatened the reliability of my results, the evaluation process yielded many surprising insights. I had been concerned about overwhelming the user with too many data dimensions, but all participants reported wanting more filtering options. This is something I would definitely include in future iterations. The survey also proved very valuable. Some participants appeared to be more willing to express they struggled to understand the visual in the survey than they were in the other evaluation formats. All participants reported having more knowledge about space object reentries after engaging with the visualization–which I think makes it a success!
